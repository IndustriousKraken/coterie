1. Pick a transactional email provider

Running your own mail server is a years-long commitment. Use a provider — they handle IP reputation, bounce processing, and the authentication machinery Gmail and friends expect.

  • Postmark — best reputation for transactional mail, 100 free/mo.
  • Resend — newer, dev-friendly, 3,000 free/mo.
  • Amazon SES — cheapest at scale, rougher UX.
  • SendGrid — big, 100/day free forever.

2. Verify your sending domain

Your provider will walk you through a domain-verification wizard. It'll give you three TXT/CNAME records to add to your DNS. Paste them into your DNS host (Cloudflare, Namecheap, Route53, etc.) and wait a few minutes for propagation. The three records are:

  • SPF — which servers are allowed to send as you.
  • DKIM — the public half of a key your provider uses to sign outbound mail.
  • DMARC — the policy receivers should use if SPF or DKIM fail, plus an address for reports.

You don't need to understand the details. Your provider's wizard generates the correct values — you just copy them in.

3. Plug the SMTP credentials in above

Your provider gives you an SMTP host, port (usually 587), username, and password. Paste them into the form, switch Mode to SMTP, save, and hit Send test email.

Common errors

authentication failed
Wrong username or password. Some providers use an API key as the password and a fixed string (e.g. "apikey") as the username.
domain not verified / sender rejected
You haven't finished the domain-verification wizard with your provider, or the From address isn't on your verified domain.
connection timed out
Wrong SMTP host, wrong port, or a firewall is blocking outbound on that port.
TLS handshake failed
Port mismatch (465 expects implicit TLS, 587 expects STARTTLS) or cert verification failure on the provider side.
